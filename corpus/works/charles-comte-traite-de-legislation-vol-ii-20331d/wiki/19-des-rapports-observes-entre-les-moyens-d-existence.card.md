## Key passage (TextRank #1)
> [p-ae0ee8] ## CHAP. 19: > Of the relations observed between the means of existence and the social state of the peoples of the Malay species of the great Ocean. — Of the kind of inequalities that exist among these peoples.The islands of the great Ocean, excepting those closest to Asia and which are connected to that continent, are divided between peoples of two species: between peoples classified under the name of the Malay species, and peoples classified under the name of the negro or Ethiopian species. As these two species of men differ from one another in their physical constitution, in the degree of intellectual development they have reached, in their morals, and in their language, it is important not to confound them. As for the peoples who inhabit the islands situated near the Asian continent, and who are classified under the name of the Mongolian species, I will make known their morals and their social state when I speak of the peoples of this species who inhabit Asia.

## Closes
> [p-b578e2] There exist, however, some differences between the peoples of these archipelagos. The individuals who belong to the subjugated class seem less numerous in some than in others; but it may be that these differences are more apparent than real. The superior chiefs, who, in all the islands, are the tallest and best-constituted men, ordinarily surround the general chief or king [570]. The navigators who landed on the principal islands must therefore have found a greater number of strong and well-constituted men than the navigators who landed on other islands [571].
