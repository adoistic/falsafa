Action occurs through the contact between sense-organs and their objects, producing perception [pratyaksha]. This is the first means of valid knowledge [pramana]. When perception reveals something possessing a particular quality or action, it generates inference [anumana]—this is the second means of valid knowledge. Comparison [upamana] arises from knowledge of similarity—this is the third means of valid knowledge. Verbal testimony [shabda] arises from knowledge of the speaker's authoritative statement—this is the fourth means of valid knowledge.

Knowledge of universals produces general awareness, while knowledge of particulars produces specific awareness. Doubt [samshaya] arises from conflicting knowledge. Correct knowledge produces satisfaction, incorrect knowledge produces dissatisfaction. Satisfaction generates desire [iccha], dissatisfaction generates aversion [dvesha]. Desire for beneficial objects produces effort [prayatna] toward attainment, aversion to harmful objects produces effort toward avoidance. Prescribed actions generate merit [dharma], prohibited actions generate demerit [adharma]. These mental impressions [samskara] of stability and motion constitute the remaining qualities.

Actions are five: throwing upward, throwing downward, contraction, expansion, and general locomotion. Universals and particulars exist as described. Inherence [samavaya] is eternal. These complete the categories of existence [bhava]. Where these are absent, non-existence [abhava] obtains...
