3

The previously mentioned six categories—substance, quality, action, universal, particular, inherence—are existents [bhava]. Where these are absent, non-existence [abhava] manifests. Now the means of valid knowledge [pramana] are described: perception [pratyaksha], inference [anumana], comparison [upamana], and verbal testimony [shabda].

Perception arises when the self, through mind as instrument, contacts sense-objects via the sense-organs. For this contact to occur, there must be conjunction between perceiver and perceived, or inherence, or conjunction with what is inherent, or inherence in what is conjoined, or conjunction with what is inherent in what is conjoined. The sensing self requires: instrumental cause [karana] as the sense-organ, material cause [upadana] as the sense-object, and efficient cause [nimitta] as the contact between them.

For visual perception of a pot, the material cause is the pot-substance, the instrumental cause is the eye-sense meeting pot-atoms through conjunction, the efficient cause is the eye-object contact producing pot-cognition. This applies to each sense-organ contacting its proper objects.
