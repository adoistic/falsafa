When smoke is perceived as pervaded by fire everywhere without exception, and this pervasive relationship [vyapti] is known, then seeing smoke on a mountain generates the inference "this mountain has fire."

Three conditions ensure valid inference: 1) The middle term [hetu] must exist in the subject [paksha]—like smoke existing in the mountain; 2) The middle term must be pervaded by the major term [sadhya] in similar instances [sapaksha]—wherever smoke exists, fire exists; 3) The middle term must be absent where the major term is absent in dissimilar instances [vipaksha]—where fire is absent, smoke is absent.

When these three conditions [trairupya] are fulfilled without contradiction [avyabhichara], valid inference results. Absence of pervasion [vyabhichara] means the middle term sometimes occurs without the major term, invalidating the inference. For example, if someone claimed "this lake has fire because it has smoke" when seeing mist, this would be invalid because mist resembles smoke but lacks the invariable connection with fire.

Knowledge of non-deviation [avyabhichara-jnana] prevents invalid inference. The absence of the middle term in negative instances [vipaksha] confirms the pervasive relationship. When doubt about pervasion [vyapti-samshaya] arises, inference becomes impossible until certainty is established.

The instrumental cause of inference is the pervasion-qualified middle term [vyapti-vishishta-hetu]. In "this mountain has fire because of smoke," the absence of smoke in fireless places and its presence only where fire exists establishes the pervasive relationship necessary for valid inferential knowledge...
